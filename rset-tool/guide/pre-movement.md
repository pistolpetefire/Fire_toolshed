# 5. Pre-movement Time

## 5.1 What Pre-movement Time Actually Is

Pre-movement time is the interval that begins when an effective cue (alarm, voice message, smoke, heat, social signal, etc.) first reaches the occupant and ends when that occupant begins **purposeful movement** toward an exit or place of safety.

It is **not** the time spent walking.  
It is the time spent recognizing that something is wrong, deciding what it means, and committing to action.

In the majority of real incidents and in the majority of published data sets, pre-movement time is the largest single component of RSET and the one with the greatest variability. Treating it as a small fixed number is one of the most common and most dangerous mistakes in performance-based design.

This tool breaks pre-movement into three observable sub-phases that appear in the research literature:

- **Perception** — the occupant becomes aware that a cue exists.  
- **Interpretation** — the occupant decides what the cue means and whether it requires action.  
- **Action** — the occupant commits to and begins purposeful movement.

These three phases are not always cleanly separable in real behaviour, but keeping them visible forces the user to confront the cognitive and social processes that actually consume time.

## 5.2 Why a Single Number Is Almost Always Wrong

Published pre-movement times show extremely wide scatter. Values measured in the same occupancy type under similar cue conditions can easily differ by a factor of three to five or more. The distribution is typically skewed: many people respond relatively quickly, while a long tail of slower responders dominates the time required to clear the last occupants.

Key factors that drive this variability include:

- Occupancy type and familiarity with the building  
- Whether occupants are awake or sleeping  
- Quality and intelligibility of the cue (tone-only vs. informative voice message)  
- Training and prior experience with drills or real alarms  
- Social influence and group decision-making  
- Presence of authority figures or staff  
- Time of day / night  
- Competing commitments (work tasks, caring for others, collecting belongings)  
- Cultural and demographic factors  

Because of this scatter, any single “typical” value is only a starting point for engineering judgment. It is never a universal constant.

## 5.3 Suggestive Values in This Tool

The tool provides scenario-based starting values drawn from the published literature. These values are **illustrative**. They are not code requirements, they are not safety factors, and they are not substitutes for project-specific analysis.

When you accept or modify a suggestive value you are required to record the basis for that decision in the Assumptions Log. The export package will show both the value used and the citation that was displayed at the time.

## 5.4 When the Suggestive Values Must Be Rejected or Adjusted

You should treat the suggestive values as inappropriate (and replace them) when any of the following apply:

- The occupancy or population characteristics differ significantly from the studies the values were drawn from.  
- The cue system is of lower (or higher) quality than assumed in the source data.  
- Occupants have special needs, are intoxicated, are very young or elderly, or have limited mobility.  
- The design is being used for a high-consequence or unusual facility (healthcare, detention, large public assembly, etc.).  
- Local data, drill results, or video analysis from similar buildings are available.  
- The Authority Having Jurisdiction or the design brief requires a more conservative (or more refined) approach.

In these cases the correct action is to replace the suggestive value with a project-specific value and document the basis.

## 5.5 Relationship to Detection and Notification

Pre-movement only begins once an **effective** cue has reached the occupant.  
If detection is slow or notification is delayed or unintelligible, the pre-movement clock has not yet started.  
Conversely, a very clear and informative voice message can shorten the interpretation phase for many people. The three segments (Detection, Notification, Pre-movement) must be considered together; optimising one while ignoring the others produces a false picture.

## 5.7 Practical Warning

A short calculated pre-movement time on a screen does not mean people will actually move that quickly.  
It means you have chosen a number.  
Your responsibility is to be able to defend that number with reference to the actual occupants, the actual cue system, and the published evidence. This tool exists to make that defence visible, not to replace it.
